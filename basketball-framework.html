The concept
What are triggers and advantages?

Scoring happens when the team is able to "trigger" an advantage by driving and making the defense collapse. Advantages are situations where we have more offensive players than defenders to guard them.

Some examples of triggers: throwing the ball ahead on the fast break, running a pick and roll, attacking off a dribble hand-off, passing to a cutter, passing to a mismatch in the post, or simply going iso and breaking down the defender. These are all situations that give us an open shot or force defenders to help — leaving someone else open.

We want to find a trigger quickly and create the advantage by making the defense collapse and scramble.

The big idea
Offense wins by finding a trigger fast (before the defense is set) and then keeping the advantage alive with quick ball movement.
Defense wins by taking away easy looks through communication and rotations — not individual heroics.
Using the code words during a game
Call out a single word from the bench — these replace the longer instructions you're currently yelling.
TRIGGER Find your action now, stop standing.
ONE MORE (or SWING) Trigger happened, someone is open — move it.
EYES UP Look ahead before you dribble — push the pace. Scan between every dribble.
ROTATE Help is needed — shift now.
TALK Communicate on defense. Self-explanatory.
Suggested practice sequence
01 Fast break chaos drill (2–3 min, every practice) — builds the eyes-up reflex. Always start here.
02 10-sec shot clock — pressures trigger-finding before the defense gets set.
03 4v3 collapse reps — builds the feel for what an advantage looks like and how to exploit it.
04 Live 5v5 with code words — coaches call from the sideline. The goal: eventually players are calling them to each other.
"Move the ball quickly" is a symptom of not understanding why to move it. Once players feel what a defensive collapse actually looks like from the 4v3 drill, the fast passing becomes instinct rather than an instruction.
